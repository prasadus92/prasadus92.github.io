Raising a $2.7M Seed Round: What Actually Worked

The honest, practical guide to raising a seed round as a first-time founder. What worked, what didn't, and what I'd do differently.

Most fundraising advice is written by people who have never actually raised money. Or worse, by people who raised money in 2021 when VCs were handing out term sheets like candy at Halloween.

This is not that article.

In 2022, I helped lead Mainteny through a $2.7M seed round as the CTO. We were building a CRM for maintenance companies in Europe. Not the sexiest market. Not a crypto play. Not AI (back then, at least). Just good old vertical SaaS in a fragmented industry that everyone else ignored.

The process took six months, 87 investor conversations, and more rejection emails than I care to count. Here's what actually happened, and what I'd tell my past self if I could.

The Reality of Fundraising as a First-Time Founder

Let me start with the uncomfortable truth that no one tells you: fundraising is a full-time job that you have to do on top of your actual full-time job.

The blog posts make it sound like a sequence of meetings. You pitch, they ask questions, you answer brilliantly, they write a check. Maybe three months, tops.

The reality? Fundraising consumed roughly 60% of my time for six months. As the CTO. The person who was supposed to be building the product. That's the first thing you need to understand: something will suffer. The question is what, and how badly.

Here's what the typical week looked like during our raise:

  • Monday-Tuesday: Back-to-back investor calls, usually 4-6 per day
  • Wednesday: Follow-ups, deck updates, data room prep
  • Thursday-Friday: Actual product work, team meetings, putting out fires
  • Weekend: Catching up on everything that slipped through the cracks

The hardest part wasn't the time. It was the context-switching. One hour you're explaining unit economics to an investor who's clearly skeptical. The next hour you're debugging a critical production issue. Then back to a pitch. Then a team one-on-one where someone needs coaching.

Your brain never fully commits to anything because it knows you're about to switch to something completely different.

"Fundraising is not a side quest. It's the main quest that runs parallel to your original main quest. Two main quests. Good luck."

If I had to do it again, I'd be more ruthless about protecting focused time. Block entire days for investor work. Block entire days for product work. The context-switching tax is real, and it's expensive.

Cold Outreach That Actually Got Responses

Here's the awkward reality of seed fundraising: unless you went to Stanford, worked at a FAANG, or already have a successful exit, you probably don't have a warm intro to most investors. We didn't.

So we did cold outreach. A lot of it.

The response rate on cold emails to VCs is brutal. Industry average is something like 2-5%. Ours was higher, around 15%, and here's why.

1. Specificity Over Generic Pitches

The worst thing you can do is send the same email to 200 investors. VCs can smell a mail merge from a mile away. They get hundreds of these per week.

Instead, we researched each investor before reaching out. What have they invested in? What do they write about? What's their thesis? Then we wrote emails that connected those dots.

Here's an example that actually worked:

Subject: Vertical SaaS in Europe's largest underserved market

Hi [Name],

I noticed your investment in [similar vertical SaaS company] and your recent post about the opportunity in European B2B markets. We're building something adjacent that might interest you.

Mainteny is a CRM for maintenance companies. It's a 500B EUR market in Europe with zero modern software players. Our 47 customers grew 3x in 8 months because the alternative is literally paper and Excel.

Would you be open to a 20-minute call next week?

[Name]

Notice what this does: it shows we did our homework, it connects to their existing interests, it gives one concrete number that makes them curious, and it asks for a small commitment (20 minutes).

2. The LinkedIn Approach

Email wasn't our only channel. LinkedIn actually had a higher response rate, around 20%, for one simple reason: it feels more personal.

The trick was to connect first with a short, non-pitchy message, then follow up with the actual pitch once they accepted. Something like:

"Hi [Name], I've been following your work on European B2B. Building something in the space and would love to connect."

No pitch. No ask. Just a human being interested in connecting. Once they accept, then you send the actual message.

3. Timing Matters More Than You Think

We tracked everything. Response rates by day of week, time of day, subject line patterns. The data was clear:

  • Tuesday and Wednesday mornings had 2x the response rate of other times
  • Emails sent Friday afternoon never got responses
  • Subject lines under 7 words outperformed longer ones
  • Follow-ups sent 4-5 days after initial email had the best conversion

This might seem like minor optimization, but when you're sending 200 cold emails, a 2x improvement in response rate means 20 more conversations. Those conversations matter.

What Investors Actually Care About

After 87 investor conversations, patterns emerge. The questions that came up again and again revealed what investors actually evaluate, regardless of what they say they evaluate.

Market Size (But Not How You Think)

Every pitch deck has a TAM slide with a big number. Investors know this number is mostly fiction. What they actually care about is whether you understand the market deeply enough to have a contrarian insight.

For Mainteny, the insight was simple: maintenance companies in Europe are large businesses (often 50-200 employees) running on paper and WhatsApp. They're not underserved because the market is small. They're underserved because they're invisible to Silicon Valley.

The investors who got excited weren't impressed by our TAM number. They were impressed that we could explain the dynamics of the market better than they'd heard before. We knew the customer intimately because we'd spent months doing nothing but talking to them.

"The best founders don't just know the market size. They know why the market exists in its current broken state, and why now is the moment it can change."

Team (The Most Honest Signal)

VCs will tell you they invest in teams. This is actually true at seed stage, but not in the way you might think.

They're not looking for impressive resumes. They're looking for founder-market fit. Why are you the person to solve this problem? What unfair advantage do you have?

For us, the story was: we had deep enterprise software experience, we'd spent months embedded with maintenance companies understanding their workflows, and we were technical founders who could build without burning through capital on an expensive engineering team.

The team questions that came up most often:

  • How did you meet your co-founder?
  • What happens when you disagree?
  • Why did you leave your previous job for this?
  • What's the hardest thing you've built together?

These questions are designed to surface dysfunction. Investors have seen too many founding teams implode. They're pattern-matching for red flags.

Product (Show, Don't Tell)

The most effective thing we did in pitches was demo the product. Not a slide about the product. The actual product.

Ten minutes of watching a real workflow in the product communicated more than any number of slides ever could. It showed we could build. It showed we understood the user. It showed the problem was real because the solution was specific.

One investor told us afterward: "I see 20 decks a week. Maybe 2 of them include a product demo. You should always demo."

The questions that followed the demo were always better, too. Instead of abstract questions about market sizing, investors asked specific questions about features, about customer feedback, about what we were building next. That's a much better conversation.

The 6-Month Timeline

Here's the actual timeline of our raise, with the emotional reality included.

Month 1: Optimism

We started with warm intros. Friends of friends, former colleagues who knew investors. These conversations went well. Lots of enthusiasm. Lots of "we should stay in touch."

We mistook politeness for interest. Rookie mistake.

Month 2: Reality Check

The warm intros dried up. We started cold outreach. Response rates were lower than expected. The conversations we did get often ended with "you're too early for us" or "we don't invest in this vertical."

This is when doubt starts to creep in. Are we fundable? Is the market real? Should we just bootstrap?

Month 3: The Grind

We refined our pitch based on feedback. Shortened the deck. Led with the demo. Got better at handling objections. Response rates improved. Conversations got deeper.

But still no term sheet. Lots of "we're interested but need to see more traction."

Month 4: First Real Interest

A Nordic VC asked for our data room. Then another. Then a third. We spent a week putting together financial models, customer contracts, team backgrounds, cap table details.

This felt like progress. It was, but it was also more work on top of everything else.

Month 5: Partner Meetings

Two firms invited us to partner meetings. This is when things get serious. You're no longer talking to one investor; you're pitching to the entire partnership.

One of those meetings went poorly. We misread the room, went too deep on technical details, and lost the non-technical partners. They passed.

The other meeting went well. They had follow-up questions. Then more questions. Then a reference call with one of our customers.

Month 6: Term Sheet and Close

The second firm sent a term sheet. This created urgency with two other investors who'd been sitting on the fence. Within two weeks, we had three term sheets to choose from.

This is the counterintuitive thing about fundraising: it's either feast or famine. No one wants to be first, but everyone wants to pile on once someone else commits.

We closed the round at $2.7M. Total time from first meeting to money in the bank: 6 months and 2 weeks.

Mistakes We Made

Looking back, we made several mistakes that cost us time, money, or both.

1. Starting Too Early

We started fundraising before we had enough traction to tell a compelling story. Our first pitch had 12 customers. By the time we closed, we had 47. If we'd waited three more months to start, the whole process would have been faster.

The math is simple: better metrics mean higher response rates, shorter due diligence, and better terms. We left money on the table by starting too early.

2. Talking to the Wrong Investors

We wasted weeks talking to investors who would never have invested in us. US firms with no European presence. Generalists who'd never done vertical SaaS. Late-stage funds doing "seed investments" that were really just options on Series A.

Better targeting upfront would have saved 30% of our time.

3. Not Having a Data Room Ready

When the first investor asked for due diligence materials, we scrambled. Financial models, contracts, cap table, team CVs, customer references. It took us a week to put together.

Have your data room ready before you start. The best opportunities move fast, and you don't want to be the bottleneck.

4. Underestimating the Emotional Toll

Rejection is part of fundraising. We knew that intellectually. But 50+ rejections over 6 months takes a psychological toll that's hard to prepare for.

There were days when I questioned everything. Was the market real? Were we good enough? Should we just give up?

Having a co-founder helped. When one of us was down, the other could keep momentum going. Fundraising alone would have been significantly harder.

The CTO's Role in Fundraising

This is something I wish I'd found more written about: what specifically should a technical co-founder do during a fundraise?

The obvious answer is "not much, focus on the product." But that's not realistic at seed stage when you have a two-person founding team.

Here's what I actually did:

The Demo

I ran every product demo. This served two purposes: it showed investors that the technical co-founder could communicate clearly, and it let the CEO handle the business questions while I handled the technical ones.

The demo became our secret weapon. Most startups at seed stage have either no product or a rough MVP they're embarrassed to show. We had a real product that solved a real problem, and I could show exactly how.

Technical Due Diligence

Some investors brought in technical advisors to evaluate our architecture. I spent hours on calls explaining our stack, our scaling strategy, our security practices.

This is where having a strong technical co-founder pays dividends. If you can't answer deep technical questions, it raises red flags.

The "Why You" Story

Part of every pitch is explaining why this team can win. As CTO, I had to articulate why our technical approach was differentiated, why we could build faster than competitors, and why our engineering culture would attract talent.

Investors want to know that the CTO has opinions about technology, not just skills. What do you believe that others don't? How does that translate into product advantages?

Keeping the Lights On

The hardest part: while doing all of the above, I also had to keep the product moving. Ship features. Fix bugs. Support customers. Maintain the infrastructure.

This is where prioritization becomes brutal. I stopped building new features almost entirely during the most intense months of fundraising. We focused on stability and quick wins that would improve our metrics for investor conversations.

Balancing Building with Fundraising

The advice you'll hear is "don't let fundraising distract from building." This is well-intentioned but not actionable.

A better framing: accept that building will slow down during fundraising, and be strategic about what you continue to build.

Here's what we prioritized:

  • Bug fixes that affected customer retention: Losing customers during a raise is catastrophic. We fixed anything that risked churn.
  • Features that improved metrics: If a feature would increase usage or conversion, we built it. These numbers show up in investor conversations.
  • Nothing speculative: We stopped all exploratory work, new integrations, experimental features. Only high-certainty, high-impact work.

We also got creative with time. I did most of my coding between 9 PM and 1 AM, after investor calls were done. Not sustainable long-term, but it got us through.

"Fundraising is a sprint, but building a company is a marathon. You need to survive the sprint without destroying your ability to run the marathon."

Would I Do It Again vs. Bootstrap?

This is the question I get asked most often. And the honest answer is: it depends.

Raising venture capital was right for Mainteny because we were in a winner-take-most market with network effects. Getting to scale fast mattered more than preserving equity. We needed capital to hire sales before we had revenue to support them.

But I've since worked on projects where bootstrapping would have been better. If you're building something where the market is large but fragmented, where being small and profitable is sustainable, where you don't need to outrun well-funded competitors, then bootstrapping gives you more options.

The calculus I'd recommend:

Raise venture capital if:

  • Speed to market is critical (network effects, winner-take-most dynamics)
  • You need significant upfront investment before revenue is possible
  • Your competitors are well-funded and moving fast
  • You're building something that requires scale to work at all

Bootstrap if:

  • You can get to profitability with minimal capital
  • The market rewards depth over breadth
  • You want to maintain control and optionality
  • Your personal financial situation allows for the slower path

The hidden cost of venture capital is the expectation it creates. Once you raise, you're on a trajectory. You need to grow fast enough to raise again, or achieve profitability at scale. The comfortable middle ground of a small, profitable business is no longer available to you.

That's a trade-off, not automatically a good or bad thing.

What I'd Tell a First-Time Founder Starting Today

If you're about to start fundraising for the first time, here's what I wish someone had told me:

1. It will take longer than you think. Double your estimate. Then add a month. Plan your runway accordingly.

2. Rejection is information, not judgment. When an investor passes, ask why. Their feedback is valuable data. Don't take it personally, but do take it seriously.

3. The best investors add more than money. We chose our lead investor partly because of their experience in European B2B SaaS. That expertise has been worth more than the extra terms we might have gotten elsewhere.

4. Fundraising is a skill. You will be bad at it initially. That's okay. You'll get better with practice. The first 20 pitches are warmup.

5. Your co-founder relationship will be tested. Fundraising stress reveals cracks in the partnership. Better to surface those issues now than after you have employees and obligations.

6. Don't optimize for valuation alone. A higher valuation with worse terms or worse investors is not a win. The goal is to set yourself up for the next 18-24 months, not to get the biggest number to brag about.

7. The market matters more than you want it to. We raised in late 2022, not the easiest time. Friends who raised in 2021 had significantly easier processes. You can't control market conditions, but you should be aware of them.

The Real Lesson

Looking back, the most important thing I learned from raising that seed round wasn't tactical. It wasn't about cold email templates or demo techniques or term sheet negotiation.

It was this: fundraising is a filter, not an accomplishment.

The hard part isn't raising the money. The hard part is building something worth investing in. If you've built real value, you'll find capital. If you haven't, no amount of fundraising tactics will save you.

The best thing you can do to make fundraising easier is to spend less time thinking about fundraising and more time building something people want. The metrics will follow. The investors will follow.

We raised $2.7M not because we were great at pitching (though we got better). We raised it because we had 47 customers who loved the product and usage metrics that showed it. Everything else was just communication.

So if you're early in your journey and stressing about fundraising: stop. Go talk to customers instead. Build something they need. The fundraising will still be hard, but it will be possible.

And if you're in the middle of a raise right now, grinding through rejection after rejection, wondering if it's worth it: keep going. The right investor is out there. They're just waiting for you to find them.
