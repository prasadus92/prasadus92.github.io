Selling Before Building at Luminik

Five months passed between my first cold LinkedIn message and Luminik's first signed customer. Most of that time was manual delivery.

In February 2025, I sent a cold LinkedIn message to a Director of Marketing at a 200-person identity verification company. In July 2025, they became Luminik's first paying customer.

I wrote the first product code only after I had spent months doing the work by hand.

Why I Did It This Way

At Mainteny, I took the familiar path. I built the MVP first, then we raised a $2.7M seed round, then we kept learning what the market actually wanted.

That path taught me something uncomfortable. Funding does not answer the questions that make a company real. It does not tell you who will buy, which channel will open the door, what language the buyer uses, where urgency comes from, or which part of the workflow is expensive enough to fix.

After Mainteny and Bain, I did not want to use a deck as a substitute for contact with reality. During SnowOptix, I approached investors too early and felt the cost of that mistake. With Luminik, I wanted the first serious evidence to come from a buyer's calendar and budget.

I also had a practical constraint. I was bootstrapping, and cash flow mattered. Manual delivery let me learn and sell in the same motion.

The First Useful Sentence

The first useful sentence did not come from me. It came from the buyer.

"Each event has its own quirks, platforms to deal with etc. So more than automation, the important thing is how we handle that."

That sentence changed the shape of the company. I had been thinking about automation. She was talking about handling. Different events, different attendee sources, different platforms, different reps, different follow-up rules. The buyer was not asking for a magic button. She wanted someone to take responsibility for the messy middle.

So I stopped trying to sound like a software vendor. I listened for the work.

The Work Before the Product

The first events were Dubai Fintech Summit and The Asian Banker Summit. I had no product to show. I had a spreadsheet, Apollo, Salesforce, HubSpot, Gamma, Slack, and a lot of manual effort.

I became the connective tissue between their GTM tools. I sat with AEs, helped shape campaigns, wrote event-specific outreach, moved contact data between systems, and reported progress back to the team. From the customer's point of view, I tried to remove work without asking them to change how they already operated.

The Slack channel mattered more than I expected. It included the VP GTM, Head of Sales, Marketing Manager, and my champion. That channel gave me the texture you never get from a demo call: which rep was overloaded, which list was late, which accounts mattered, which event was politically important inside the company.

What Being Embedded Taught Me

One month they had five events. I was supporting multiple AEs at the same time. The work was too much for one person, which was exactly the point.

I learned where the work repeated and where it did not. Data movement repeated. ICP matching repeated. First-draft outreach repeated. Reporting repeated. Judgment did not repeat as cleanly. The team still needed to decide which accounts mattered, which meetings were worth pushing for, how aggressive the outreach should feel, and when a conversation was worth an AE's time.

That distinction became the product boundary. Automate the repetitive parts. Keep the human judgment visible.

From Pilot to Contract

By July, the work had stopped being speculative. The customer had seen me operate across real events, inside their real tools, with their real team. When the contract was signed, my champion wrote that they would not have had an event strategy without my help.

That sentence mattered because it named the value correctly. The value was not a dashboard. It was an operating system for an event motion that had previously depended on heroic coordination.

When Software Became Obvious

I wrote the first code after two to three months of hands-on delivery. By then I had lived with the workflow enough to know which parts deserved software.

If I had built first, I would have designed from imagination. I would have overbuilt the clean parts and underbuilt the annoying parts. The annoying parts are where products usually live.

Luminik is still being built from that posture. The backend automation came first because it reduced delivery load immediately. The customer-facing UI follows the same rule: build the surfaces that make the workflow clearer, faster, and easier to trust.

The Rule I Use Now

Technical founders often reach for code because code gives the day shape. Sales days feel blurrier. You send messages, wait, listen, follow up, and hear no many times before the work sharpens.

I still prefer building. The difference is that I now treat manual delivery as part of building. It builds the judgment layer before the codebase hardens.

The operating rule
  1. Find a buyer with a live operational problem.
  2. Listen for the words they use when the workflow breaks.
  3. Do the work manually inside their current process.
  4. Notice which steps repeat across events, reps, and tools.
  5. Charge once the manual work is clearly useful.
  6. Write software around the repeated work.

What I Would Tell a Technical Founder

If you can sell a manual version of the outcome, you learn faster than a prototype can teach you. You also learn which promises you are willing to be accountable for.

That accountability is useful. It keeps the product honest.

If you are trying to decide whether to build first or sell first, my bias is now simple: sell the outcome manually until the repeated work becomes impossible to ignore.

Prasad Subrahmanya

Founder & CEO at Luminik. Previously built Aura at Bain ($3.6M ARR) and led Mainteny ($2.7M seed). Building Luminik, the event pipeline platform for B2B teams.
