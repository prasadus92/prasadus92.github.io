            At Mainteny, one prospect asked how our system would handle a scheduling edge case in their maintenance workflow.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            The question was too specific for a feature-list demo. It touched the data model, technician availability, recurring jobs, and what happened when a customer changed a request after dispatch.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            I could answer because I had built that part of the product. I explained the current behavior, the tradeoff behind it, and a limitation they would hit if they used the workflow in a certain way.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            That call changed how I thought about sales. Technical depth is useful when it helps the buyer understand their own workflow more clearly.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            The advantage is not charisma. The advantage is diagnosis.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">Why Technical Founders Underrate This Skill</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            Many technical founders dislike sales because they associate it with pressure, vague promises, and performance. I had the same reaction for years.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            The better version feels closer to engineering.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            A buyer describes a workflow. You ask for the current state. You find the constraint. You test whether your product fits. You explain the tradeoffs. You write down what you heard and what happens next.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Engineers can be good at this because the work rewards precision.
        </p>

        <blockquote class="reveal">
            "The best salespeople don't sell. They help people buy."<br>
            <span style="font-style: normal; color: var(--color-text-muted);">Josh Braun</span>
        </blockquote>

        <p class="reveal">
            When I was at Bain building Aura, I watched how the most successful internal sales happened. It wasn't through flashy presentations or clever persuasion. It was through deeply understanding someone's problem, showing them exactly how the solution worked, and being honest about what it could and couldn't do. The partners who adopted Aura most enthusiastically were the ones who felt genuinely helped, not sold to.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">Why Deep Product Knowledge Beats Sales Techniques</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            I had a call at Mainteny that showed this clearly. The prospect was evaluating us against two competitors. He'd already talked to their sales teams and received polished demos with all the usual bells and whistles.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            During our call, he asked a technical question about how our system would handle a specific edge case in their workflow. It was the kind of detailed question that usually gets a "Let me get back to you on that" response.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            But I built that part of the system. So I walked him through exactly how it worked, why we designed it that way, and what the tradeoffs were. I also pointed out a limitation he hadn't asked about but would definitely encounter given his use case. Then I suggested a workaround.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            He signed the contract that week. Later, he told me the competitors' sales reps couldn't answer his technical questions and had to "check with the product team." That delay and uncertainty cost them the deal.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            This is the practical advantage technical founders have. You don't need to check with anyone. You are the product team. You can go deeper on technical questions than any salesperson who's memorizing feature lists.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            More importantly, you can have real conversations about architecture, integrations, and implementation. In B2B software, those conversations often matter more than anything else. Your buyer is buying a solution to a technical problem. Having someone who genuinely understands that problem on the other side of the table changes everything.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">Treat the Call as Diagnosis</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            The mental shift was simple: stop treating sales calls as opportunities to pitch. Treat them as opportunities to understand.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Your job is to help the buyer figure out whether what you have is a fit for what they need. If it is, the next step becomes obvious. If it is not, that is also useful information.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            This approach feels natural to engineers because it's basically how we approach problem-solving anyway. You start by understanding the problem. You consider different solutions. You weigh tradeoffs. You make a recommendation based on the specific context.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            On a sales call, this looks like asking genuine questions about their situation, listening carefully to the answers, and then honestly mapping your solution to their needs, including being upfront about gaps.
        </p>

        <div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0;">
            <h4 style="margin-bottom: 16px;">The Consultative Mindset</h4>
            <ul style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); list-style: none; padding: 0; margin: 0;">
                <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;"><strong>Instead of:</strong> "Let me show you our features"</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;"><strong>Try:</strong> "Tell me about what you're trying to accomplish"</li>
                <li style="border-top: 1px solid var(--glass-border); padding-top: 12px; margin-top: 12px;"></li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;"><strong>Instead of:</strong> "Our product does X, Y, and Z"</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;"><strong>Try:</strong> "Based on what you've described, we could help with X. Y might not be the right fit, but let me explain why."</li>
                <li style="border-top: 1px solid var(--glass-border); padding-top: 12px; margin-top: 12px;"></li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 12px;"><strong>Instead of:</strong> "What would it take to close this deal today?"</li>
                <li><strong>Try:</strong> "Does it make sense to keep talking, or is this not a priority right now?"</li>
            </ul>
        </div>

        <p class="reveal">
            This approach increases trust. When you give people permission to say no, they listen more carefully. When you are honest about limitations, they believe you when you talk about strengths. When the product is a poor fit, you learn that early.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">How Engineers Naturally Do Discovery Well</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            Discovery is the process of understanding a prospect's situation before proposing a solution. It is where most sales processes break down. Traditional salespeople often rush through it to get to the pitch. They ask a few token questions, then launch into their demo script.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Engineers, by contrast, are almost pathologically inclined toward thorough discovery. We want to understand the problem before suggesting solutions. We're uncomfortable making recommendations without sufficient context. We ask follow-up questions because we genuinely want to understand the edge cases.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            That thoroughness is an advantage.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Good discovery questions from a technical founder might include:
        </p>

        <ul class="reveal">
            <li>"Walk me through how you're handling this today"</li>
            <li>"What does your current tech stack look like?"</li>
            <li>"Where does the process break down?"</li>
            <li>"What have you tried that didn't work?"</li>
            <li>"If this worked well six months from now, what would be different?"</li>
            <li>"Who else is involved in making this decision?"</li>
            <li>"What's driving the timeline on this?"</li>
        </ul>

        <p class="reveal">
            The key is asking these questions with genuine curiosity, not as a checklist. When someone describes their current workflow, you should be naturally interested in understanding it deeply. That interest comes through and builds trust.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            I've found that the best sales conversations feel like technical design discussions. You're both trying to figure out the right solution to a real problem. Sometimes that solution is your product. Sometimes it's something else. Either way, you've had a valuable conversation.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">Handling Objections with Technical Honesty</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            Traditional sales training teaches you to "overcome" objections with clever rebuttals. Engineers often struggle with this because it feels manipulative. We can see through our own tricks.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            You don't need tricks. Honesty is more effective.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            When a prospect raises an objection, they're telling you something important about their needs or concerns. The worst thing you can do is dismiss it or spin it away. Instead, take it seriously.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            If the objection is valid, acknowledge it directly. Then do three things: explain why that limitation exists, describe what you're doing about it (if anything), and help them evaluate whether it's a dealbreaker for their specific situation.
        </p>

        <blockquote class="reveal">
            "Prospects don't trust salespeople who have an answer for everything. They trust salespeople who are honest about tradeoffs."
        </blockquote>

        <p class="reveal">
            At Luminik, I've had calls where prospects asked about features we don't have yet. Instead of pretending we're "working on it" (the classic sales dodge), I tell them exactly where it is on our roadmap and why other things are prioritized higher. Sometimes they're fine waiting. Sometimes it's a dealbreaker. Either outcome is better than setting false expectations.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Technical founders are particularly well-positioned for this kind of honesty because we understand the engineering tradeoffs behind product decisions. We can explain not just what the product does, but why it does it that way. That depth of understanding builds credibility that no sales technique can replicate.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">The Power of "I Don't Know, But I'll Find Out"</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            Saying "I don't know" does not hurt your credibility when you follow through with the answer.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            When you pretend to know something you don't, people can usually tell. Even if they can't tell immediately, they'll find out eventually. And then they won't trust anything else you've told them.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            But when you say "That's a great question, and I'm not sure about the answer. Let me find out and get back to you" and then actually follow through, you demonstrate integrity. You've shown that you won't make things up to close a deal. That's rare and valuable.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            As a technical founder, you'll know more about your product than any salesperson could. But you still won't know everything. You might not know the specifics of a particular integration. You might not know whether your system can handle a specific edge case until you test it. You might not know the exact pricing for an unusual configuration.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            In all these cases, "I don't know, but I'll find out" is the right answer. And the follow-through matters: send them the answer within 24 hours, ideally with more detail than they expected. This becomes a trust-building moment rather than a credibility gap.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">Practical Tactics for Technical Founders Doing Sales</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            Alright, let's get practical. Here are the specific tactics that have worked for me:
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Structure your calls</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Don't wing it. Have a loose structure: opening (build rapport, set agenda), discovery (understand their situation), demo/discussion (show relevant parts of your solution), and next steps (agree on what happens next). The structure keeps you on track while leaving room for genuine conversation.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Demo less, talk more</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Engineers love showing off what they've built. Resist this urge. Spend more time in discovery and less time in demo. When you do demo, only show the parts relevant to their specific use case. A targeted 10-minute demo beats a comprehensive 45-minute feature tour every time.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Take detailed notes</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Write down what they tell you during discovery. Not just for your CRM, but so you can reference their exact words later in the conversation. "Earlier you mentioned that X was a problem. This is how we handle it" is much more powerful than generic feature descriptions.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Send a summary after every call</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Within 24 hours, email them a summary of what you discussed, what you understood their needs to be, and the agreed next steps. This demonstrates professionalism and creates accountability. It also gives them something to share with other stakeholders.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Use the "negative close"</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Instead of pushing for a commitment, give them an easy out: "Based on what we've discussed, do you think this is worth continuing, or is it not the right fit?" This removes pressure and paradoxically makes them more likely to say yes if they're genuinely interested.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Follow up without being annoying</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Many engineers are so afraid of being pushy that they don't follow up at all. That's a mistake. People are busy; they forget. A useful follow-up is specific: "I looked back at the workflow we discussed. The main open question seems to be whether the operations team owns this handoff or sales does. Is that the right thing to resolve next?" Follow up when you have a useful next thought, not because a sequence told you to appear in their inbox.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Get comfortable with silence</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            After you ask a question, wait for the answer. Don't fill the silence. This is hard for many people, but silence often prompts prospects to share more information than they initially planned to.
        </p>

        <h3 class="reveal">Record your calls (with permission)</h3>
        <p class="reveal">
            Review them later. You'll notice patterns: questions you ask poorly, moments where you talked too much, objections you could have handled better. This is how you improve.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">When to Keep Selling vs. When to Hire</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            The question every technical founder eventually faces: when should I stop doing sales myself and hire someone?
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            The conventional wisdom says "as soon as possible." I think that's wrong.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Founder-led sales has enormous advantages in the early stages. You learn directly from customers. You understand objections firsthand. You can make product decisions based on what you're hearing in sales calls. You build relationships with early customers that will be valuable for years.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            More practically, you also haven't figured out your sales motion yet. What's the ideal customer profile? What messaging resonates? What's the right price point? What objections come up repeatedly? What's the typical sales cycle? You need to answer these questions before you can hire someone to execute a process that doesn't exist yet.
        </p>

        <div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0;">
            <h4 style="margin-bottom: 16px;">Keep doing sales yourself if:</h4>
            <ul style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); list-style: none; padding: 0; margin: 0;">
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">You haven't closed at least 10-15 customers yourself</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">You can't articulate a repeatable sales process</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">Your ICP is still evolving</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">Your pricing isn't stable</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">Sales cycles are long and require deep technical credibility</li>
                <li>You're not yet overwhelmed by the sales workload</li>
            </ul>
        </div>

        <div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0;">
            <h4 style="margin-bottom: 16px;">Consider hiring when:</h4>
            <ul style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); list-style: none; padding: 0; margin: 0;">
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">You have a documented, repeatable sales process</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">Sales is consuming more than 50% of your time</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">The product needs more attention than sales does</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">You have enough pipeline that deals are falling through the cracks</li>
                <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;">You can afford to pay a good salesperson properly</li>
                <li>You have clear metrics to evaluate sales performance</li>
            </ul>
        </div>

        <p class="reveal">
            Even then, your first sales hire probably shouldn't be a quota-carrying rep. Consider a sales development rep (SDR) who handles outbound and qualification, leaving you to do the actual selling. Or a customer success person who handles post-sale work, freeing you up for more prospects. The goal is to extend your capacity, not replace yourself entirely.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            When you do hire a salesperson, look for someone who matches your consultative approach. The pushy, "always be closing" types will feel wrong to you and will alienate the customers you've been building relationships with. Find someone who genuinely wants to help customers solve problems, not just hit quota.
        </p>

        <h2 class="reveal">The Practical Advantage</h2>

        <p class="reveal">
            It took me years to accept that my discomfort with traditional sales tactics was useful. Modern B2B buyers do not need theater. They need help making a good decision.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Buyers don't want to be sold to. They want to be helped. They want to talk to someone who understands their problem deeply and can have an honest conversation about solutions. They want technical credibility, not charisma.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            As a technical founder, you have all of that. You just need to stop thinking you need to be something else.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            The engineer who deeply understands the product, asks thoughtful questions, gives honest answers, and follows up reliably will outsell the smooth-talking rep who's working from a script. The reason is trust.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Trust compounds. The prospect you helped today, even if they didn't buy, remembers that experience. They might come back later when the timing is right. They might refer a colleague. They might become a customer at their next company.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            Sales is building relationships with people who have problems you can solve. Engineers are good at solving problems. Turns out we can be good at building those relationships too.
        </p>

        <p class="reveal">
            The standard is practical: diagnose the workflow, explain the tradeoffs, write the follow-up, and keep selling until the pattern is repeatable.
        </p>


        <p class="reveal" style="font-style: italic; color: var(--color-text-tertiary);">
            These lessons come from Mainteny, Bain, and now Luminik. Technical founders do not need to become salespeople in costume. They need to bring clarity, curiosity, and earned product judgment into the room.
        </p>
