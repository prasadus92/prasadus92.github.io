<div class="reveal">
  During Mainteny's seed round, the work was making the company legible to investors who had lived outside it. Product decisions, customer proof, market structure, founder fit, technical risk, and the live demo all had to compress into a story an investor could understand quickly and stress-test under diligence.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The argument in one paragraph">
  Fundraising is narrative work backed by a real product. The hard part is not raising money. The hard part is building something worth investing in. The best thing a founder can do to make fundraising easier is spend less time on fundraising and more time on the company.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">Investor Outreach That Respects Attention</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Warm introductions help. A sharp thesis still does the work. Cold outreach can work when it is researched, specific, and concise. The best messages did three things quickly: showed investor fit, made the market legible, and gave one reason the team had earned the right to build the company.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  The worst thing you can do is send the same email to 200 investors. They can smell a mail merge. Read their portfolio. Read their writing. Connect specific dots.
</div>

<Callout tone="info" title="The cold-outreach structure that worked">
  <p><strong>Subject:</strong> Vertical SaaS in an overlooked operational market</p>
  <p>Hi <em>[Name]</em>,</p>
  <p>I noticed your investment in <em>[similar company]</em> and your recent post about European B2B markets. We are building something adjacent.</p>
  <p><em>[Company]</em> helps an operationally complex market move from fragmented workflows to a purpose-built system of record. We have early customer proof, a clear wedge, and a product that can be shown live.</p>
  <p>Would you be open to a 20-minute call next week?</p>
</Callout>

<div class="reveal">
  Four things matter in that template: it shows you did the homework, it connects to a thesis they have already publicly committed to, it anchors the company in a specific market, and it asks for a small commitment.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">Diligence: Where Vague Storytelling Collapses</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Financial model, customer proof, contracts, cap table, references, technical architecture, hiring plan. The story has to survive contact with the documents. Once one high-conviction investor leans in, the market changes. The same company can feel speculative one week and obvious the next. Be ready before that moment: clean data room, crisp references, clear terms, and a product story that does not need constant rescue.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The CTO's Role During the Raise</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  A technical co-founder does four things during a raise that the CEO cannot do alone.
</div>

<Callout tone="note" title="What the CTO owns during a seed">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Run the demo.</strong> Shows the technical co-founder can communicate. Lets the CEO handle the business questions while you handle the technical ones.</li>
    <li><strong>Handle technical due diligence.</strong> Some investors bring advisors. Be able to explain trade-offs clearly, including where the system is intentionally simple.</li>
    <li><strong>Articulate the "why you" technical story.</strong> Why is your approach differentiated? Why can you build faster than competitors? Investors want a CTO with opinions about technology, not just skills.</li>
    <li><strong>Keep the product alive.</strong> During the intense months, focus on stability, demo quality, and the product improvements that make the company stronger in customer conversations.</li>
  </ul>
</Callout>

<h2 class="reveal">What I Would Tighten Next Time</h2>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>Pressure-test the narrative before starting.</strong> Wedge, expansion story, demo, objection handling. The raise is too late to discover the gaps.</li>
  <li><strong>Target investor fit more ruthlessly.</strong> Some firms are structurally unlikely to invest no matter how good the conversation feels. Stage, geography, check size, category experience, and partner ownership all matter. A pleasant conversation with the wrong firm is still a cost.</li>
  <li><strong>Prepare diligence before anyone asks.</strong> Treat the data room as the operating system of the raise, with every claim, metric, and assumption easy to inspect.</li>
  <li><strong>Protect founder attention.</strong> Process beats willpower. Clear ownership, weekly review of investor status, and enough product time that the company keeps improving while the round is in motion.</li>
</ul>

<KeyTakeaway label="What stayed with me">
  Raising capital is useful when it improves the company's odds of doing the work ahead. It is dangerous when it becomes the work. If you have built real value, you will find capital. If you have not, no fundraising tactics will save you.
</KeyTakeaway>
