<div class="reveal">
  The round had many moving parts. My part, as founder and CTO, was to make the product real, make customer evidence inspectable, explain the technical choices plainly, and keep learning from sales calls while the round was in motion.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  That work mattered because the sales motion was still early. Measured against the seed expectations a founder would face in 2026, the round could not rest on a mature sales machine or a long revenue history. The product had to carry more of the evidence. Mainteny later raised $2.7M, and the live product, customer conversations, and technical story helped make the company easier to believe.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The argument in one paragraph">
  Fundraising gets clearer when the company is already worth inspecting. The CTO's role is to make that inspection honest: show the product, know the customer workflow, explain the system, name the risks, and protect enough product time that the company keeps improving during the raise.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">The product has to carry part of the pitch</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  A deck can explain the market. A live product shows whether the team can turn market understanding into software. In early fundraising, that distinction matters because investors are trying to answer a simple question: can this team learn faster than the market expects?
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  My job was to make the demo survive real questions. If an investor asked how scheduling worked, I could show it. If they asked how a technician saw their jobs, I could explain the mobile flow. If they asked where the system was still simple, I could say so without dressing it up.
</div>

<PullQuote>
  The best fundraising conversations felt like accurate explanation. The strong parts were specific. The weak parts were visible. The next risks had names.
</PullQuote>

<h2 class="reveal">Customer calls are part of diligence</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Investors listen differently when the technical founder has been in customer conversations. You hear the buyer's language directly. You know which objections are real. You can tell the difference between a product gap that blocks a deal and a feature idea that can wait.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  At Mainteny, early customer and sales calls changed how I talked about the product. We were building field service management software for elevator maintenance companies, and the workflow was more than dispatch. Scheduling, quotations, technician updates, invoicing, and customer visibility all decided whether the office looked organized or lost control of the day.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="What customer calls gave the raise">
  Customer conversations turned the company story from "we are building software for maintenance teams" into a sharper claim: we understand how elevator jobs move through the business, where the workflow breaks, and which first version customers will actually try.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">Investor outreach rewards real homework</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  The outreach that worked was researched and short. It opened with a specific reason the investor might care, then gave the company in plain language, then made a small ask.
</div>

<Callout tone="info" title="A safe version of the structure">
  <p><strong>Subject:</strong> Field service software for elevator maintenance</p>
  <p>Hi <em>[Name]</em>,</p>
  <p>I saw your firm had backed software for operational businesses with field teams. We are building in a related market.</p>
  <p>Mainteny helps elevator maintenance companies run the workflow from request to quotation, technician work, invoicing, and customer visibility.</p>
  <p>The product is live enough to show the workflow, and the early customer conversations are shaping what we build next. If this fits your thesis, I would value 20 minutes of advice on the market and the next commercial evidence we should earn.</p>
</Callout>

<div class="reveal">
  The message works because it respects attention. It gives the investor a reason to keep reading and gives them enough truth to decide whether the conversation belongs on their calendar.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The CTO owns technical credibility</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Technical credibility during a raise means explaining why the system is shaped the way it is, which risks are real, and which decisions were intentionally postponed.

<h2 class="reveal">Diligence punishes vague claims</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Diligence is where a story meets documents. Product screenshots, customer references, contracts, financial model, cap table, technical notes, hiring plan, and demo access all need to tell the same story.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  The CTO should prepare the product side before the first serious investor asks. Write down the architecture in plain English. Keep a clean list of known limitations. Make sure the demo account works. Prepare answers for security, data handling, reliability, and what will change after funding.
</div>

<Callout tone="note" title="CTO diligence checklist">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Demo path.</strong> One reliable flow that proves the product's daily value.</li>
    <li><strong>Architecture note.</strong> The stack, data model, deployment setup, and the reasons behind each choice.</li>
    <li><strong>Risk list.</strong> What is fragile today, what gets fixed with capital, and what can safely wait.</li>
    <li><strong>Customer evidence.</strong> What customers tried, what they asked for, what changed because of it.</li>
    <li><strong>Roadmap logic.</strong> The next product bets and why they follow from customer evidence.</li>
  </ul>
</Callout>

<h2 class="reveal">Protect the company while the round is moving</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Fundraising can eat the company if every founder spends the week reacting to meetings, follow-ups, deck edits, and diligence requests. The product still needs to get better. Customers still need support. Bugs still compound.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">Good investor fit feels calm</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  The best investor conversations required clarity. A good investor asked better questions, understood the stage, respected what was still unknown, and helped sharpen the company without pushing it into a story that did not fit.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  A bad fit usually showed up in the questions. If the investor wanted a different company, a different market, or a level of certainty the stage could not support, the right answer was to move on. Capital is useful when it increases the odds of doing the work. It is expensive when it forces the company into a shape the founders do not believe in.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">What I would do again</h2>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>Put the product in the room early.</strong> A real workflow changes the tone of the meeting.</li>
  <li><strong>Join customer calls.</strong> The CTO should know the commercial objections firsthand.</li>
  <li><strong>Tell the truth plainly.</strong> Investors can handle early-stage risk. They lose trust when the risk is hidden.</li>
  <li><strong>Separate fundraising work from product work.</strong> Context switching makes both worse.</li>
  <li><strong>Choose investor fit carefully.</strong> A seed investor becomes part of the company's operating reality.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="reveal">What I would tighten next time</h2>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>Prepare the data room earlier.</strong> It is easier to improve the story when the underlying material is already organized.</li>
  <li><strong>Write the technical memo before diligence.</strong> A clear memo reduces repeated explanations and exposes weak thinking early.</li>
  <li><strong>Track investor objections more systematically.</strong> Repeated objections are market feedback. One-off objections are usually taste.</li>
  <li><strong>Keep the outreach list narrower.</strong> A small group of well-fit investors beats a large list that creates false motion.</li>
</ul>

<KeyTakeaway label="What stayed with me">
  Fundraising is easier when the CTO behaves like a business owner. Build the product, sell through the workflow, explain the technical choices, prepare the evidence, and protect the company's ability to keep learning.
</KeyTakeaway>
