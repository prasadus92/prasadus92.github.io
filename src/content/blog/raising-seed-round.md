<p class="reveal">
    During Mainteny's seed round, the work was making the company legible to people who had not lived inside it.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That meant turning product decisions, customer proof, market structure, founder fit, technical risk, and the live demo into a story an investor could understand quickly and test under diligence.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The Time Cost</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Fundraising is not a side quest. It competes directly with product work, customer work, hiring, and operating cadence.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The only rhythm that worked was separating the modes:
</p>

<div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0;">
    <ul style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); list-style: none; padding: 0; margin: 0;">
        <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;"><strong>Investor blocks:</strong> calls, follow-ups, objection notes, and narrative updates.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;"><strong>Product blocks:</strong> customer-impacting work, demo quality, and metric movement.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;"><strong>Diligence blocks:</strong> contracts, financial model, cap table, references, and technical notes.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 8px;"><strong>Recovery blocks:</strong> time to think clearly before the next conversation.</li>
    </ul>
</div>

<p class="reveal">
    The hardest part is context-switching. One hour you are explaining market structure. The next hour you are deciding what to ship. Then you are back inside a diligence thread. Without mode separation, every part of the company gets a shallower version of your attention.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Block entire days for investor work. Block entire days for product work. The context-switching tax is real.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">Investor Outreach That Respects Attention</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Warm introductions help, but they do not replace a sharp thesis. Cold outreach can work when it is researched, specific, and concise.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The best messages did three things quickly: showed investor fit, made the market legible, and gave one reason the team had earned the right to build the company.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Research Each Investor</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    The worst thing you can do is send the same email to 200 investors. They can smell a mail merge.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    What have they invested in? What do they write about? What is their thesis? Connect those dots.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The structure that worked:
</p>

<div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0; font-family: var(--font-mono); font-size: 0.9rem;">
    <p style="margin-bottom: 12px; color: var(--color-text-secondary);">Subject: Vertical SaaS in an overlooked operational market</p>
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 12px;">Hi [Name],</p>
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 12px;">I noticed your investment in [similar company] and your recent post about European B2B markets. We are building something adjacent.</p>
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 12px;">[Company] helps an operationally complex market move from fragmented workflows to a purpose-built system of record. We have early customer proof, a clear wedge, and a product that can be shown live.</p>
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 12px;">Would you be open to a 20-minute call next week?</p>
</div>

<p class="reveal">
    This shows you did homework, connects to their interests, anchors the company in a specific market, and asks for a small commitment.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">LinkedIn Works Better</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    LinkedIn can work when the message still respects the same bar: specific, relevant, and easy to ignore without guilt.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Connect first with a non-pitchy message:
</p>

<div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0; font-family: var(--font-mono); font-size: 0.9rem;">
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary);">"Hi [Name], I have been following your work on European B2B. Building something in the space and would like to connect."</p>
</div>

<p class="reveal">
    Do not fake intimacy. Earn the second message by making the first one relevant.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Timing</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    We tracked everything. The pattern was simple:
</p>

<ul class="reveal">
    <li>Short messages beat complete explanations.</li>
    <li>Investor fit beat volume.</li>
    <li>Follow-up worked when it added new information.</li>
    <li>The demo mattered more than the deck once a meeting started.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="reveal">What Investors Actually Evaluate</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    After enough conversations, patterns emerge.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Market Understanding</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    Every pitch deck has a TAM slide with a big number. Investors know this number is fiction.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    What they care about: do you understand the market deeply enough to have a contrarian insight?
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The TAM number impressed nobody. What got investors excited was that we could explain market dynamics better than they had heard before. We knew the customer because we had spent months talking to them.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Team</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    At seed stage, investors evaluate founder-market fit. Why are you the person to solve this problem? What context do you have that another team would need years to earn?
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Questions that came up most:
</p>

<ul class="reveal">
    <li>How did you meet your co-founder?</li>
    <li>What happens when you disagree?</li>
    <li>Why did you leave your previous job for this?</li>
    <li>What is the hardest thing you have built together?</li>
</ul>

<p class="reveal">
    These questions test whether the founding team can handle pressure, disagreement, and ambiguity without turning inward.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Product Demo</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    The most effective thing we did: demo the product. Not a slide about the product. The actual product.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Ten minutes of watching a real workflow communicated more than any slides. It showed we could build. It showed we understood the user. It showed the problem was real because the solution was specific.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The lesson was simple: if the product is real, let the product carry part of the pitch.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">How the Narrative Improved</h2>

<h3 class="reveal">First Version: Too Much Explanation</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    The first version of the pitch explained too much. We wanted investors to understand the whole product, the whole market, and every technical decision. That made the story feel slower than the company actually was.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Polite interest is not momentum. Momentum is specific next steps, diligence questions, reference calls, and a partner who starts doing work on the opportunity.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Second Version: Sharper Wedge</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    The second version made the wedge clearer. We stopped trying to prove the whole market in one conversation and focused on the narrow customer pain we knew better than anyone else in the room.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That changed the quality of the questions. Investors moved from "what is this?" to "how big can this become?"
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Third Version: Demo First</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    The third version led with the demo earlier. Once investors saw the workflow, the market stopped feeling abstract. The product made the pain concrete.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That gave us a better conversation. We could talk about buyer behavior, expansion, and product velocity instead of spending the entire meeting defining the category.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Diligence: Proof Beats Persuasion</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    Diligence is where vague storytelling collapses. Financial model, customer proof, contracts, cap table, references, technical architecture, hiring plan: the story has to survive contact with the documents.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Partner Meetings: Translate the Company</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    In partner meetings, you are no longer only convincing the person who liked you first. You are helping them make the case internally. That means the narrative must travel without you.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The CTO has a specific job here: make technical depth legible without making the company feel narrow. The architecture matters, but only because it explains why the company can move faster, serve the customer better, or build a compounding advantage.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    A good partner meeting creates better questions, not silence. It should leave the room with a sharper version of your company than the one they had before.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Closing: Momentum Compounds</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    Once one high-conviction investor leans in, the market changes. The same company can feel speculative one week and obvious the next. That is not entirely rational, but it is how venture markets often behave.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The founder's job is to be ready before that moment: clean data room, crisp references, clear terms, and a product story that does not need constant rescue.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">What I Would Tighten</h2>

<h3 class="reveal">Raise From a Stronger Narrative Base</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    Metrics matter, but narrative readiness matters too. Before starting a raise, I would pressure-test the wedge, the expansion story, the demo, and the objection handling more aggressively.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Target Fit More Ruthlessly</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    Some investors are structurally unlikely to invest no matter how good the conversation feels. Stage, geography, check size, category experience, and partner ownership matter. A pleasant conversation with the wrong firm is still a cost.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Better targeting preserves attention for the investors who can actually lead the round.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Prepare Diligence Before Anyone Asks</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    The data room is not clerical work. It is the operating system of the raise: financial model, contracts, cap table, team background, customer proof, references, and technical notes. When a good investor asks, the answer should be ready.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Have your data room ready before you start. Good opportunities move fast.
</p>

<h3 class="reveal">Protect Founder Attention</h3>

<p class="reveal">
    Fundraising creates a dangerous kind of ambient pressure. Every conversation feels important. Every delayed reply invites interpretation. The way through is process: clear ownership, weekly review of investor status, and enough product time that the company keeps improving while the round is in motion.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The CTO's Role</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    What should a technical co-founder do during a raise?
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>Run the demo.</strong> Shows the technical co-founder can communicate. Lets the CEO handle business questions while you handle technical ones.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>Handle technical due diligence.</strong> Some investors bring in advisors to evaluate your architecture. The CTO should be able to explain tradeoffs clearly, including where the system is intentionally simple.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>Articulate the "why you" story.</strong> Why is your technical approach differentiated? Why can you build faster than competitors? Investors want a CTO with opinions about technology, not just skills.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>Keep the product alive.</strong> During the intense months, I focused on stability, demo quality, and product improvements that made the company stronger in customer conversations.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">Balancing Building with Fundraising</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Accept that building slows down during fundraising. Be strategic about what you continue.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    What we prioritized:
</p>

<ul class="reveal">
    <li><strong>Bug fixes affecting retention:</strong> Losing customers during a raise is catastrophic.</li>
    <li><strong>Features that improved metrics:</strong> These numbers show up in investor conversations.</li>
    <li><strong>Nothing speculative:</strong> No exploratory work, new integrations, or experiments. Only high-certainty, high-impact work.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="reveal">Raise vs Bootstrap</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Should you raise at all?
</p>

<div class="glass-card reveal" style="padding: 24px; margin: 24px 0;">
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 16px;"><strong>Raise if:</strong></p>
    <ul style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 24px;">
        <li>Speed to market is critical (network effects, winner-take-most)</li>
        <li>You need significant upfront investment before revenue is possible</li>
        <li>Competitors are well-funded and moving fast</li>
        <li>The product requires scale to work at all</li>
    </ul>
    <p style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 16px;"><strong>Bootstrap if:</strong></p>
    <ul style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); margin-bottom: 0;">
        <li>You can get to profitability with minimal capital</li>
        <li>The market rewards depth over breadth</li>
        <li>You want to maintain control</li>
        <li>Your financial situation allows for the slower path</li>
    </ul>
</div>

<p class="reveal">
    The hidden cost of venture capital: once you raise, you are on a trajectory. You need to grow fast enough to raise again, or achieve profitability at scale. The comfortable middle ground of a small, profitable business is no longer available.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">What I Would Tell a First-Time Founder</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>1. It takes longer than you think.</strong> Double your estimate. Then add a month.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>2. Rejection is data.</strong> When an investor passes, ask why. Do not take it personally, but take it seriously.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>3. The best investors add more than money.</strong> Their expertise can be worth more than better terms elsewhere.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>4. Fundraising is a skill.</strong> The pitch improves when you treat every conversation as feedback on clarity, not as a referendum on the company.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>5. Your co-founder relationship will be tested.</strong> Fundraising stress reveals cracks in the partnership. Better now than after you have employees.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>6. Do not optimize for valuation alone.</strong> A higher valuation with worse terms or worse investors is not a win.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    <strong>7. Market conditions matter.</strong> You cannot control them, but be aware of them.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">What Stayed With Me</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Raising capital is useful when it improves the company's odds of doing the work ahead. It is dangerous when it becomes the work.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The hard part is not raising money. The hard part is building something worth investing in. If you have built real value, you will find capital. If you have not, no fundraising tactics will save you.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The best thing you can do to make fundraising easier is to spend less time thinking about fundraising and more time building something people want.
</p>


<p class="reveal" style="font-style: italic; color: var(--color-text-tertiary);">
    The best fundraising work happens before the raise starts: build proof, sharpen the wedge, and make the company easy to understand without making it small.
</p>
