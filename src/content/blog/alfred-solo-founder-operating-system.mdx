<div class="reveal">
  I run Luminik on my own. The product, the customers, the GTM, and the operating model all live in one head. That works at low scope. It stops working the moment "recurring company work" becomes a real category of its own.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  Alfred is my answer. It is a small fleet of role contracts around work that needs to happen repeatedly, with a review surface where outputs land and one founder who still owns every consequential decision. This post explains the reusable shape: what each role needs to know, what it can produce, and the parts I am turning into an OSS version.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The model in one paragraph">
  Alfred is a set of role contracts around recurring company work. The public lesson is the contract: approved context, reviewable output, source-linked claims, and human approval before anything consequential ships. The private runtime, exact surfaces, schedules, and routing stay out of the public version.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">The Five Questions a Role Has to Answer</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Before any role goes live, it has to answer five questions in writing. The questions live as the role's instruction file. If any answer is vague, the role is not ready.
</div>

<ol class="reveal">
  <li><strong>What context can it read?</strong> Specific files, repos, public docs, and approved private notes required for the task.</li>
  <li><strong>What action can it take?</strong> Search, summarize, draft, open a PR, or post to a review surface. Never publish, send, pay, merge, or deploy.</li>
  <li><strong>Where does the output land?</strong> A pull request, a review note, a markdown file, or a digest. Everything has a path I can find without asking.</li>
  <li><strong>What evidence does it provide?</strong> Source links, PR numbers, log lines, file paths. A claim with no provenance is a bug.</li>
  <li><strong>What requires my approval?</strong> The line between draft and ship. I want this line obvious enough that a tired founder cannot accidentally cross it.</li>
</ol>

<div class="reveal">
  I use the same five questions to retire roles. Two early agents got cut because I could not write a clean answer to question two. They were doing too much.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">Why Solo Builders Need This More</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  A team has imperfect safeguards that still help: standups, code reviews, review threads, shared calendars, peer correction. A solo builder has none of those by default. The upside is speed. The downside is that every gap routes back to one person.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  Agents do not remove the need for operating design. They make operating design more important. When the work gets cheaper to generate, the review queue becomes the scarce resource.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">What the OSS Version Should Contain</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Alfred is getting an OSS version around the parts that transfer cleanly to other solo founders. The private version has company context, customer data, and adapters that stay private. The public version should focus on the reusable scaffolding: contracts, review gates, output shapes, and a small runner pattern.
</div>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>Role contracts.</strong> Markdown templates that answer the five questions for chief of staff, content, sales, ops, and engineering subagent roles.</li>
  <li><strong>Repo instruction templates.</strong> Example CLAUDE.md, AGENTS.md, and content-guide files for solo product teams.</li>
  <li><strong>Review rules.</strong> The approval gates I use for shipping code, content, sales replies, and finance actions.</li>
  <li><strong>Output formats.</strong> Concise summaries, source-linked digests, PR notes, and the morning briefing shape.</li>
  <li><strong>A local runner shape.</strong> Enough structure for scheduled jobs and reviewable output without my private adapters.</li>
</ul>

<div class="reveal">
  The release should be useful before it is complete. A solo founder should be able to adopt the role contracts inside Claude Code, Cursor, or any agent harness they prefer, without taking my private adapters with them.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The standard I use now">
  Alfred is good when its work is reviewable, source-linked, and bounded. It is bad when I notice it deciding things I should be deciding. Solo builders need recurring work to stay visible and reviewable while the founder remains responsible for the decisions that matter.
</KeyTakeaway>
