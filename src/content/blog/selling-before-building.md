<p class="reveal">
    In Luminik's early discovery, I did not start with a product walkthrough. I started with a narrow outbound message to a marketing leader whose team cared deeply about events.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The work became real before the software did. I spent enough time inside the workflow to see which parts repeated, which parts required judgment, and what a buyer would actually trust.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">Why I Did It This Way</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    At Mainteny, I took the familiar path. I built the MVP first, then we raised a $2.7M seed round, then we kept learning what the market actually wanted.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That path taught me something uncomfortable. Funding does not answer the questions that make a company real. It does not tell you who will buy, which channel will open the door, what language the buyer uses, where urgency comes from, or which part of the workflow is expensive enough to fix.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    After Mainteny and Bain, I did not want to use a deck as a substitute for contact with reality. During SnowOptix, I approached investors too early and felt the cost of that mistake. With Luminik, I wanted the first serious evidence to come from a buyer's calendar and budget.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    I also had a practical constraint. I was bootstrapping, and cash flow mattered. Manual delivery let me learn and sell in the same motion.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The First Useful Sentence</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    The first useful sentence did not come from me. It came from the buyer.
</p>

<blockquote class="reveal">
    The buyer kept returning to the same idea: every event had its own quirks, and the real value was not automation by itself. The value was handling the messy middle well.
</blockquote>

<p class="reveal">
    That changed the shape of the company. I had been thinking about automation. She was talking about handling. Different events, different attendee sources, different platforms, different reps, different follow-up rules. The buyer was not asking for a one-click feature. She wanted someone to take responsibility for the messy middle.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    So I stopped trying to sound like a software vendor. I listened for the work.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The Work Before the Product</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    The first events were real, time-bound, and operationally messy. I had no software surface yet. I had the customer's existing tools, a spreadsheet, and a lot of manual effort.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    I became the connective tissue between their GTM tools. I sat close to the sales motion, helped shape campaigns, wrote event-specific outreach, moved contact data between systems, and reported progress back to the team. From the customer's point of view, I tried to remove work without asking them to change how they already operated.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    The shared working rhythm mattered more than I expected. It gave me the texture you never get from a demo call: where the team was overloaded, which lists arrived late, which accounts mattered, and which event had internal urgency.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">What Being Embedded Taught Me</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    At one point, several events overlapped and the work had to coordinate across sales stakeholders at the same time. It was too much for one person to keep doing manually, which was exactly the point.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    I learned where the work repeated and where it did not. Data movement repeated. ICP matching repeated. First-draft outreach repeated. Reporting repeated. Judgment did not repeat as cleanly. The team still needed to decide which accounts mattered, which meetings were worth pushing for, how aggressive the outreach should feel, and when a conversation was worth an AE's time.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That distinction became the product boundary. Automate the repetitive parts. Keep the human judgment visible.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">From Pilot to Contract</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    By July, the work had stopped being speculative. The customer had seen me operate across real events, inside their real tools, with their real team. When the contract was signed, the value was already felt because the product promise had been delivered manually first.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That sentence mattered because it named the value correctly. The value was not a dashboard. It was an operating system for an event motion that had previously depended on too much coordination.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">When Software Became Obvious</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    I wrote the first code after enough hands-on delivery to understand the workflow. By then I had lived with the work long enough to know which parts deserved software.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    If I had built first, I would have designed from imagination. I would have overbuilt the clean parts and underbuilt the annoying parts. The annoying parts are where products usually live.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    Luminik is still being built from that posture. The backend automation came first because it reduced delivery load immediately. The customer-facing UI follows the same rule: build the surfaces that make the workflow clearer, faster, and easier to trust.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The Rule I Use Now</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    Technical founders often reach for code because code gives the day shape. Sales days feel blurrier. You send messages, wait, listen, follow up, and hear no many times before the work sharpens.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    I still prefer building. The difference is that I now treat manual delivery as part of building. It builds the judgment layer before the codebase hardens.
</p>

<div class="post-visual reveal">
    <div class="post-visual-title">The operating rule</div>
    <ol style="color: var(--color-text-secondary); padding-left: 20px; margin: 0;">
        <li>Find a buyer with a live operational problem.</li>
        <li>Listen for the words they use when the workflow breaks.</li>
        <li>Do the work manually inside their current process.</li>
        <li>Notice which steps repeat across events, reps, and tools.</li>
        <li>Charge once the manual work is clearly useful.</li>
        <li>Write software around the repeated work.</li>
    </ol>
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">What I Would Tell a Technical Founder</h2>

<p class="reveal">
    If you can sell a manual version of the outcome, you learn faster than a prototype can teach you. You also learn which promises you are willing to be accountable for.
</p>

<p class="reveal">
    That accountability is useful. It keeps the product honest.
</p>


<p class="reveal" style="font-style: italic; color: var(--color-text-tertiary);">
    If you are trying to decide whether to build first or sell first, my bias is now simple: sell the outcome manually until the repeated work becomes impossible to ignore.
</p>
