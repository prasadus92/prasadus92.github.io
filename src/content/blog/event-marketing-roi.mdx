<div class="reveal">
  Event ROI is a CRM problem disguised as a reporting problem. By the time the CMO asks what an event produced, the evidence is already scattered across attendee portals, enrichment tools, sequencers, badge scanners, AE notes, and Salesforce. The dashboard is weak because the data model was weak from the start.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The argument in one paragraph">
  A serious event motion runs through five handoffs: source, enrich, sequence, capture, attribute. If one handoff drops the shared identifier, the final report becomes a reconstruction. Fix the handoffs and the dashboard becomes trivial. Fix the dashboard without fixing the handoffs and you keep paying for events you cannot defend.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">Two ledgers, only one of them clean</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Finance forces the cost side to be clean. Booth, sponsorship, travel, dinners, shipping, contractors, design. Every line has an owner and an invoice. The signal side is messier because no single team owns it end to end.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The five handoffs that actually decide ROI</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  This is the work behind a defensible report. Each stage owes the next stage a piece of evidence. The shared record is what makes the chain work.
</div>

<div class="role-grid">
  <RoleCard
    name="Source"
    role="Stage one"
    trigger="Event lands in the GTM calendar"
    reads="Approved public pages and customer-provided event records"
    writes="A target list with provenance"
    guardrail="Use public or customer-provided records with provenance."
  >
    <p>Names the universe. The output is who might be there. Without provenance, the next four stages cannot defend their inputs.</p>
  </RoleCard>

  <RoleCard
    name="Enrich"
    role="Stage two"
    trigger="Target list ready"
    reads="Customer-approved enrichment source"
    writes="ICP-scored contact records in the CRM"
    guardrail="Use only tools and data sources the customer has approved."
  >
    <p>Names who matters. ICP fit, role, seniority, account state in CRM. The cost of enriching the wrong list is everything that comes after.</p>
  </RoleCard>

  <RoleCard
    name="Sequence"
    role="Stage three"
    trigger="ICP-scored list landed"
    reads="Customer-approved sequencer or CRM context"
    writes="Email plus LinkedIn sequences scoped to the event"
    guardrail="Never auto-replies. Reps still own the relationship."
  >
    <p>Names who was actually contacted. The signal worth tracking is reply rate by ICP segment, not raw open count.</p>
  </RoleCard>

  <RoleCard
    name="Capture"
    role="Stage four"
    trigger="Floor opens"
    reads="Customer-provided meeting notes and scan records"
    writes="Event-specific contact and account records, offline-tolerant"
    guardrail="Same-day CRM sync. Notes attach to the right opportunity, not a generic lead bucket."
  >
    <p>Names what happened on the floor. The handoff that breaks most often, because it depends on humans capturing context while a venue is loud.</p>
  </RoleCard>

  <RoleCard
    name="Attribute"
    role="Stage five"
    trigger="After the floor closes"
    reads="Customer-approved CRM opportunity context"
    writes="Sourced and influenced pipeline tagged to the event record"
    guardrail="Confidence level on every match. Never silently overwrite an existing source."
  >
    <p>Names what changed in CRM. Sourced pipeline. Influenced pipeline. New opps. Accelerated opps. Each one with a confidence level a CFO can audit.</p>
  </RoleCard>
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">Why the booth scan is already too late</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  A badge scan is not the start of event data. For a pipeline-focused team, the scan is the middle. The first three stages decide whether the booth is a checkpoint inside a larger pipeline motion or just a lead-collection exercise.
</div>

<PullQuote attribution="What the manual delivery taught me">
  A target-account choice changes the enrichment priority. An enrichment result changes the outreach angle. A meeting note changes the follow-up. A follow-up changes the attribution story in CRM. None of that survives if every stage is talking to a different system.
</PullQuote>

<h2 class="reveal">What a defensible report actually measures</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  The CMO does not need more rows. The CMO needs a small set of numbers a CFO can interrogate without flinching.
</div>

<Callout tone="info" title="Where AI actually helps in this workflow">
  Models are good at the text-heavy pieces: classifying companies from messy event pages, summarizing AE meeting notes into structured records, drafting first-pass outreach scoped to a buyer's role. They should not decide event strategy. They prepare evidence so humans can decide faster.
</Callout>

<h2 class="reveal">The practical fix</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  Give every event a single operating record. Attach targets, lists, sequences, meetings, scans, notes, and CRM outcomes to that record. Use the enrichment and sequencer contracts the team already pays for. Keep Salesforce or HubSpot as the place where the final pipeline story lives.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  Then measure the event as a pipeline motion, not a pile of leads. Pre-booked meetings, target-account coverage, opportunity influence, sourced pipeline, confidence level. Those are the numbers a CMO can defend.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The shape of the fix">
  Preserve the evidence while the work is happening. If you cannot point to one event record that holds source, enrich, sequence, capture, and attribute together, the ROI conversation keeps ending the same way every quarter.
</KeyTakeaway>
