<p class="reveal">
  In early 2021 I quit my job to build a CRM for maintenance companies in Europe. Three months later there was a working product, three paying customers, and the conviction to raise a $2.7M seed round. No 16-hour days. No productivity hack. The scope was small enough that one person could build, sell, and support it without pretending the company was larger than it was.
</p>

<KeyTakeaway label="The argument in one paragraph">
  An MVP is not a smaller version of the product. It is the smallest thing that proves the business is viable. Being the only developer is an advantage during this phase, because the bottleneck of an early company is judgment, and one technical brain holding the entire codebase makes judgment cheap.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">Why Being the Only Developer Helped</h2>

<p class="reveal">
  At Mainteny I had a co-founder running the business while I owned the entire technical stack. Common shape for an early company: one on sales, partnerships, operations; one building. Plenty of people will tell you a single-developer setup is risky. They are right at scale. They are wrong at MVP.
</p>

<PullQuote>
  In the MVP phase, speed is useful only when it helps you learn from customers sooner.
</PullQuote>

<p class="reveal">
  One-person dev teams are not better long term. They are fragile. Build the engineering team after the concept is proven. In the early uncertain months, having one technical brain making all the calls eliminates coordination overhead entirely.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">Scoping Ruthlessly: The Art of Strategic Omission</h2>

<p class="reveal">
  The hardest skill in building an MVP is deciding what to leave out. Mainteny's customers were HVAC technicians, plumbers, and electricians. The full software those businesses need is large: scheduling, dispatching, inventory, invoicing, customer communication, reporting. A complete solution would take years. I had three months. So I asked one question obsessively: <strong>what is the one thing that, if it works, proves the business is viable?</strong>
</p>

<p class="reveal">
  For Mainteny the answer was scheduling and job management. Everything else got cut: no inventory, no invoicing integration, no mobile app, no fancy reporting, no permissions beyond admin and technician.
</p>

<Callout tone="info" title="The MVP scoping framework">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Must have.</strong> The core value proposition. Without this, the product has no reason to exist.</li>
    <li><strong>Should have.</strong> Features that make the core significantly better. Build in month two or three if time permits.</li>
    <li><strong>Could have.</strong> Nice additions that do not affect the core hypothesis. Do not touch them.</li>
    <li><strong>Won't have.</strong> Everything else. Write them down so you stop thinking about them.</li>
  </ul>
</Callout>

<p class="reveal">
  The won't-have list is the most important document. My Mainteny version had over 40 items. Every time a customer mentioned a feature, it went onto that list instead of the roadmap. Customers respected this. They preferred a focused tool over a bloated mess.
</p>

<p class="reveal">

<h3 class="reveal">What I deliberately deferred</h3>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>Advanced permissions.</strong> The first customers needed clear ownership, not an enterprise policy engine.</li>
  <li><strong>Deep analytics.</strong> Enough usage signal to learn, not a dashboard pretending the product was mature.</li>
  <li><strong>Complex automation.</strong> Manual steps were acceptable when they helped me understand the workflow better.</li>
  <li><strong>Native mobile.</strong> The first usage pattern was operational and desktop-led, so mobile could follow the evidence.</li>
  <li><strong>Multi-market polish.</strong> One market, one workflow, one narrow buyer pain.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="reveal">Tech Stack: Boring Beats Better</h2>

<p class="reveal">
  When you are building alone, the temptation to try new technologies is strong. Resist it. This is not the time to learn Rust or experiment with a new database. I chose the most boring stack I knew well.
</p>

<Callout tone="info" title="The MVP tech stack">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Backend.</strong> Spring Boot 2.x with Spring Data JPA. Years of muscle memory.</li>
    <li><strong>Database.</strong> PostgreSQL, managed.</li>
    <li><strong>Frontend.</strong> Server-rendered templates plus JavaScript. One codebase, not two.</li>
    <li><strong>Hosting.</strong> Kubernetes on Google Cloud. Faster than a VM because I had deployed dozens of apps to it.</li>
    <li><strong>CI/CD.</strong> GitHub Actions plus simple deployment scripts.</li>
  </ul>
</Callout>

<PullQuote>
  Choose technologies based on your speed with them, not their theoretical fit. A technology you know deeply beats a technology that is "better" but unfamiliar.
</PullQuote>

<h2 class="reveal">Selling While Building</h2>

<p class="reveal">
  The biggest mistake technical founders make is building in isolation. They spend months coding, then discover they have built the wrong thing. I started selling in week 3, before the product was usable. The pitch was specific and respectful: <em>I am building a tool for businesses like yours. Can I show you what I am working on and get your feedback?</em>
</p>

<p class="reveal">
  Enough people said yes. Those conversations did three things at once: validated the problem, shaped the product, and seeded the customer pipeline. By the time the MVP was ready, fifteen companies were waiting to try it. Three became paying customers in the first month.
</p>

<KeyTakeaway label="What early customers actually want">
  Early customers do not expect perfection. They expect seriousness. If their feedback turns into visible product improvement, the relationship changes from vendor evaluation to co-creation. Be honest about what works, what is immature, and how quickly you respond when something blocks them.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">The Fundraising Inflection Point</h2>

<p class="reveal">
  With three paying customers and a working MVP, I had what investors needed to see: a market that existed, an ability to build, and customers who would pay. The seed round came together quickly. Investors could talk to real users and look at real usage data. A working product changed the conversation more than another slide could.
</p>

<h2 class="reveal">The Solo Developer Advantage</h2>

<p class="reveal">
  Building an MVP as the only developer is about moving fast during the phase where speed matters most. Once you have validated the concept, build the engineering team. Once you have product-market fit, scale the organization. In those early uncertain months, having one person who can pivot the architecture instantly, ship daily, and iterate without coordination overhead is a genuine advantage.
</p>

<KeyTakeaway label="The point was never that one developer should stay alone forever">
  Three months, one developer, one narrow product, three paying customers were enough to prove the idea deserved more ambition. The first proof did not require an organization. It required a narrow problem, direct customer contact, and enough engineering judgment to turn each customer objection into the next product decision.
</KeyTakeaway>
