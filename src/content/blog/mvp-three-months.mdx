<div class="reveal">
  Mainteny was field service management software for elevator maintenance companies. The first useful version was built in three months because we made it live inside one daily workflow: office teams needed to schedule jobs, send technicians, issue quotations, invoice customers, and keep customers informed without losing the thread.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  I wrote the first product myself and joined the commercial work. I sat in customer calls, watched objections turn into product decisions, and learned which parts of the workflow buyers cared about enough to change their habits.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The argument in one paragraph">
  The first version had one job: make a real elevator maintenance company better at daily work. That meant choosing one market, one workflow, and one practical architecture. The product that came out of that work made the company easier to understand: here was the workflow, here was the software, here were the limits, and here was what customers were teaching us.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">The customer was concrete</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  The first customers were maintenance businesses with technicians in the field, office staff handling calls, and building customers waiting for updates. One early customer exposed the product problem better than any generic market map could.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  A job could start as a phone call, turn into a quotation, become a technician visit, require a follow-up part, and end with an invoice or a customer-dashboard update. If the software lost any step, the office still needed paper, spreadsheets, WhatsApp, or memory to close the gap.
</div>

<PullQuote>
  A first product earns trust when it removes one daily irritation without asking the customer to pretend the rest of the company is already automated.
</PullQuote>

<h2 class="reveal">The first scope was deliberately narrow</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  The tempting version of the product was huge: scheduling, dispatch, quotations, invoicing, customer dashboards, inventory, customer communication, permissions, reporting, mobile apps, and integrations. The useful first version was smaller. It handled the work from request to invoice and left the rest visible.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">The technical choices were boring on purpose</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  I used tools I could move fast with and debug alone. The product needed to be reliable enough that customer calls produced product learning instead of apologies.
</div>

<Callout tone="info" title="The first stack">
  <ul>
    <li><strong>Backend.</strong> Kotlin and Quarkus for the API.</li>
    <li><strong>Frontend.</strong> React for the web app used by office teams.</li>
    <li><strong>Mobile.</strong> React Native for technicians in the field.</li>
    <li><strong>Database.</strong> PostgreSQL, including custom indexing for the first search experience.</li>
    <li><strong>Search.</strong> Custom Postgres-backed search first, Elasticsearch later when the product needed it.</li>
    <li><strong>Infrastructure.</strong> AWS, with managed services where they saved time and custom code where the workflow needed it.</li>
    <li><strong>Third-party tools.</strong> Used selectively for jobs like invoice PDF generation when building everything from scratch would slow customer learning.</li>
  </ul>
</Callout>

<div class="reveal">
  The stack worked because every layer had a job. Quarkus and Kotlin gave me a typed backend with fast iteration. React kept the office workflow easy to change. React Native let technicians update work without waiting for a separate product cycle. Postgres carried the first product further than many people expect.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">Sales calls changed the product</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  The customer conversations were part of product work. A maintenance owner would describe a dispatch problem. A technician would explain why a status field was wrong. An office manager would ask what happened when a customer changed the appointment after the technician had already been assigned.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  Those questions tested whether I understood the work. When I could answer with the actual behavior of the product, or say clearly what it did not handle yet, the conversation became more honest.
</div>

<KeyTakeaway label="The sales lesson">
  Technical founders have an advantage when they sell through the workflow. You can hear a customer objection and know whether it is a missing button, a weak data model, a bad promise, or a market signal the product should leave alone.
</KeyTakeaway>

<h2 class="reveal">The weekly rhythm protected customer learning</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  I treated the week as four blocks: build, customer calls, review, buffer. Mornings were for building. Customer calls and follow-ups had dedicated time. Planning happened after the customer work had created new evidence.
</div>

<h2 class="reveal">What I would keep</h2>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>One narrow market.</strong> Elevator maintenance companies had enough shared workflow shape that learning transferred from one conversation to the next.</li>
  <li><strong>One complete daily path.</strong> Request to invoice was more useful than five half-built modules.</li>
  <li><strong>Direct customer contact.</strong> The technical decisions were better because I heard the commercial objections myself.</li>
  <li><strong>A practical stack.</strong> I chose tools that made debugging and change cheap.</li>
  <li><strong>Honest demos.</strong> Customers trusted clear limits more than polished answers that collapsed in real use.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="reveal">What I would change</h2>

<ul class="reveal">
  <li><strong>Start customer calls even earlier.</strong> Some architecture choices would have been sharper if I had heard the field workflow before writing the first version of the data model.</li>
  <li><strong>Instrument usage from day one.</strong> Support calls tell one part of the story. Usage data shows where the product quietly fails.</li>
  <li><strong>Test pricing sooner.</strong> Price tells you what kind of pain the buyer thinks you solve.</li>
  <li><strong>Write the deferred list more formally.</strong> A visible list of postponed features helps customers understand focus without feeling ignored.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="reveal">The lesson I still use</h2>

<div class="reveal">
  A first customer version is part product and part market test. It teaches the market, makes sales conversations more concrete, and gives investors something real to inspect.
</div>

<div class="reveal">
  The useful skill is knowing which part of the business must become software first. At Mainteny, the answer came from customer calls, elevator field-service workflows, and a stack I could change quickly when the truth changed.
</div>
